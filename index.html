INSECTS

Insects are the largest group within the animal kingdom, with over a million described species and many more yet to be discovered. They belong to the class Insecta and are characterized by a three-part body structure: the head, thorax, and abdomen. Insects typically have six legs, antennae, compound eyes, and an exoskeleton made of chitin.

Insects play a crucial role in ecosystems. Many, like bees and butterflies, are pollinators, helping plants reproduce. Others, such as ants and termites, contribute to decomposition by breaking down organic matter. Some, like mosquitoes and flies, can transmit diseases, while others, like ladybugs and praying mantises, help control pest populations.

1. Green Orchid Bee (Euglossa dilemma)


The Dilemma Orchid Bee, otherwise known as the Green Orchid Bee, is native to Central America but has also been spotted in Florida.

The bee, sporting a shiny metallic green hue, grows to about half an inch. The females collect pollen with baskets on their hind legs. The males, however, focus on attracting mates with fragrances from specific orchids, thus playing a critical role in pollination.



Surprisingly, despite stings, their bite is less severe than the common honeybee's.

2} the bettle of egypt

European Honey Bee (Apis mellifera)

The European honey bee, also known as the Western honey bee, holds a unique place in history. This insect was domesticated some five millennia ago. To this day, beekeepers maintain this species for honey production and pollination. Thanks to human aid, it now thrives on every continent, Antarctica aside.


Unlike its bee counterparts, the western honey bee boasts year-round colonies, which makes it a superorganism. Here, the colony, not the individual, takes precedence in reproduction.


Interestingly, these bees have a modified ovipositor or stinger. Workers and queens wield it in hive defense. A popular misconception is that bees invariably die post-sting. In reality, the bee's mortality hinges on the victim, generally surviving unless it stings a mammal

3) Wheel Bug (Arilus cristatus)

The Wheel Bug is an impressive assassin bug. It's the only one of its kind found in the US. It ranks among North America's largest terrestrial true bugs, sizing up to 1.5 inches.


Its grey-toned camouflage merges well with leafy havens. It's often sighted amidst sunflower fields, cotton plants, or tree groves. Remarkably, its flight resembles a grasshopper's.


Organic gardeners value these types of bugs. They prey on various pests, proving an ecosystem's health. As entomologist Dr. Raupp states, they're the "lion" of your garden's food web - a testament to landscape vitality.

4) Twelve-spotted skimmer

Twelve-spotted Skimmers are fast and agile. If given time to count, one would see a total of twelve black spots on all four wings of the male - three on each one. Between these black spots sit eight bright white spots that may even appear light blue. White smudges also appear next to the body. The female and immature males have the twelve brown-black spots, but not the white ones, so it is still acceptable to call them all twelve-spotted. Males have a powdery blue abdomen or 'tail'. The female tail is mostly black with a long yellow stripe on each side. Adults feed on small flying insects.


This species is common across all three countries on the North American continent. They are active mostly in the summer and are fast fliers. Look for these large, robust skimmers near the water's edge by lagoons, creeks, ponds, and lakes

5} Leaf Beetles (Chrysomelids)


Leaf beetles form a diverse group known for their ovular or rounded bodies. Uniquely, their antennae aren't overly long, and their eyes exhibit no notching. Their exterior displays various aesthetics, from dull hues to bright, patterned displays or metallic tones.



These beetles, adults and larvae alike, have a broad plant-based diet. Serious pest issues arise with species like Colorado potato, asparagus, cereal leaf beetle, mustard beetle, and flea beetle. However, some leaf beetles contribute positively by controlling invasive weeds.

6} Atlas Moth (Attacus atlas)

The Atlas moth inhabits tropical and subtropical rainforests, with a significant presence in Southeast Asia. They have an impressive wingspan of nearly 10 inches and rank among the world's largest moths. Size variations exist between genders, with females tending to be larger.


Their wings, deep orange to brown at the base, display an intricate mix of white, black, pink, and brown. A white triangular spot accents the center of each wing. Noticeably, a distinctive protrusion extends from the anterior distal edge of the forewing.

The tan to brown cocoons of these types of insects are particularly notable. Their material bears a resemblance to the silk of silk moths. In fact, in East Asia, they weave this silk into a naturally brown textile known as fagara.

7} Termite (Isoptera)

Found worldwide except Antarctica, termites' species diversity peaks in South America. Misconstrued as "wood ants" or "white ants" in older texts, they are not actually ants.

These insects are eusocial species that feed on decaying plant matter, including wood, leaf litter, and soil. Eyes absent in most worker and soldier termites, their residences rise from unconventional resources such as feces, used plant material, and soil. These architectural wonders can be aboveground carton nests or concealed tropical subterranean domiciles.

An unfortunate intersection with humans emerges from their wood-eating trait, causing significant damage to wooden structures. Alarmingly, around 83 out of 3,106 species are perceived as destructive11, with an astounding $1.5 billion in annual infrastructure damage in the southwestern U.S. alone.

8} Silverfish (Lepisma saccharinum)


Silverfish, a nocturnal insect known for its fish-like shape, lives mostly in the dark. This insect transforms from a white hatchling to a metallic, grayish adult. Key features include two long cerci, a terminal filament, and, in a few species, small eyes.


Lacking in wings, the silverfish displays a wiggling, fish-like locomotion. Its unique ability to digest cellulose comes from the cellulase-producing midgut, allowing it to consume a range of polysaccharide-rich materials.


These bugs consume and damage a myriad of substances, from book bindings and carpets to adhesives and dandruff. As a result, they are considered household pests. Their redeeming trait is that they do not transmit diseases.

9} Glowworm beetle (Phengodidae)


The Glowworm beetle is an interesting creature. The females exhibit bioluminescence, resembling their larval stage. They cast light through photic organs on each body segment and sometimes from luminous bands extending across the body. These females are spotted more frequently than the larvae.



On the other hand, male glowworm beetles have standard beetle-like features, their elaborate antenna standing out as their unique trait. They use these 'feathers' to trail female pheromones.

10} Tiger beetle (Cicindelinae)


Tiger beetles thrive in sandy habitats across many continents, notably the United States. They're nimble creatures, quick to take flight and have a near-perfect vision. Their bright hues adorn their bodies, earning them the label "butterflies of the beetle world."


Each of these types of bugs brandishes bulging eyes, slender legs, and curved mandibles.
